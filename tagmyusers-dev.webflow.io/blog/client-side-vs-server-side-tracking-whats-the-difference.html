What's the difference between client-side and server-side tracking?

Are you ready for a world without ad pixels and third-party cookies? Are you prepared for a world without client-side tracking?

You should be, because it’s coming fast and might break your online marketing efforts.

Brands need to collect and track data about their customers. No data collection means no analytics, no attribution, no insights, no growth.

Since the beginning of the Internet, brands have relied on client-side tracking to collect data and make their marketing investments profitable. 

Things like ad pixels, tags, and third-party cookies have been the foundations of how tracking is done for more than two decades.

But what if everything was about to change? Would your bottom line take a hit or would you be able to adapt and thrive?

What Is Client Side Tracking and Why It’s Going Away

To collect data on your website, you need to track what users do by recording key user activities directly on their browsers. That’s called client-side tracking.

Key information collected in the browser would typically be page views, clicks, UTM parameters for campaign attribution, conversions, etc.

Everything is collected directly from the browser, using technologies like marketing pixels, analytics tags, third-party cookies, etc.

Usually, you set up a tag management system like Google Tag Manager. There you can configure JavaScript scripts to be loaded on your website depending on specific conditions.

When triggered, these scripts, also called tags or pixels, will execute inside the user’s browser, and send the required information to their servers.

For example, the Meta pixel can send an Add to Cart event from the browser to Meta servers to be processed. Technically the information is sent as an HTTP request.

In addition to sending HTTP requests to servers, client-side tracking will also need to save information between page views. That’s done using cookies. Cookies are simple text files stored in the browser that can persist information for up to 400 days.

Analytics solutions like Google Analytics often use first-party cookies. First-party cookies only work on one root domain and its subdomains. For example, for a root domain like example.com, subdomains would be domains like us.example.com, mx.example.com, or france.example.com.

In the case of ad networks like Google, Meta, or Snapchat, user information is often saved using third-party cookies.

Third-party cookies can be used across different websites/domain names and are useful for a broad set of use cases, like retargeting.

In the following illustration from Wikipedia, a fictional ad company placed banners on two websites. By hosting the banner images on its servers and using a third-party cookie named QWERTY, the ad company can track user activity across these two sites.

Client-Side Tracking Has a Lot of Advantages

Client-side tracking has been the main way to collect user data on websites for more than two decades because it brings a lot of benefits to the table. Here are some of these benefits:

It’s comprehensive

Client-side tracking happening right inside the browser means that you have direct access to a vast amount of user data. You have access to the URLs visited by the user, to the pages’ content, to the user interactions with the content, to the characteristics of the browser and the device, to the user location, and more.

It’s cheap

Client-side tracking works inside the user’s browser, so you don’t have computing costs to worry about. The JavaScript scripts, tags, or pixels are usually hosted by the vendors themselves on their servers or by your tag management system.

Having been the default way to do things for a long time, client-side tracking already has a vast ecosystem of vendors and experts you can rely on to implement what you need.

It’s fast

Since you don’t have to host anything yourself, the tracking implementation work is usually straightforward and quick to complete.

Well, is there a catch?

With those benefits, you might ask yourself something... If client-side tracking is comprehensive, cheap, fast, already there and working, why would we need to change anything? Unfortunately, one key development has shifted the calculus.

‍The drawbacks now outweigh the benefits. 

The Problem: The Disadvantages of Client-Tracking Are Getting Too Big To Ignore

Everything is NOT fine with client-side tracking.

Over the last decade, users’ preferences have progressively moved towards more privacy and better ways to safeguard that privacy.

These new preferences have brought changes in the regulatory landscape. These changes make pure client-side tracking non-viable in the long run.

Recent regulations have been promulgated that limit what can be tracked and how it can be done.

In the European Union, we have the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) and the ePrivacy Directive. In the US, we have the California Consumer Privacy Act (CCPA) among others. In the UK there’s the Data Protection Act 2018.

This combined wave of users’ demands regarding privacy and new regulations has prompted major browsers to restrict client-side tracking.

Apple’s Safari designed the Intelligent Tracking Prevention (ITP) mechanism. Firefox created the Enhanced Tracking Protection feature. 

Somewhat reluctantly, Google has also outlined a plan to phase out third-party cookies in Google Chrome by 2025…

Here are the formidable challenges you’ll face with your client-side tracking from now on.

Ad blockers

Globally, one-third of Internet users use ad blockers. So they can not be reliably tracked using only client-side tracking.

Browsers’ restrictions

Safari and Firefox have banned third-party cookies making pure client-side attribution and retargeting almost impossible. Google Chrome plans to follow suit in 2025 after having postponed the decision several times. Safari and Firefox have also implemented some restrictions to first-party cookies for certain types of use cases.

Regulatory compliance

Client-side tracking, where you execute uncontrolled scripts from third parties in the user’s browser, can expose you to data leaks.

These third-party scripts, once loaded in the browser, can harvest information in a way that’s not compliant with the privacy regulations you abide by. 

This can cause you a lot of trouble, and make you lose a lot of money.

Another challenge you’ll have to deal with is the performance nosedive inflicted by the ever-increasing number of tags, pixels, and scripts you load on your website.

Various tags loading on the BestBuy website

Over time, all these tags in the browser, each collecting information and sending requests to vendors’ servers degrade the performance of your website. A slower, less responsive website will mean a worse user experience and can hurt your sales.

All these drawbacks point in one direction: you cannot rely on a pure client-side approach for your tracking anymore.‍

The drawbacks now nullify the benefits.

So what can be done? That’s where server-side tracking comes in.

What Is Server-Side Tracking and Why It’s the Future of Tracking

Instead of sending browser information directly to third-party servers, the core idea of server-side tracking is to collect user information first in a server you control. From that server, you can decide where to relay this information and how to do it.

This approach has many advantages and allows you to deal with the challenges making client-side tracking untenable in the long term. Going server-side is a great way to make your tracking future-proof.

In practice you’ll still keep a small part of client-side tracking to collect the information that can only be collected in the browser. But instead of having all your tags client-side sending data to third-party servers, you’ll keep the absolute minimum of tags client-side and put most of your tags on a server.

User data collected on the browser will be sent to your server first, before being dispatched to third parties.

The Right Solution to the Problem: The Advantages of Server-Side Tracking

Let’s see how server-side tracking helps you address the issues plaguing client-side tracking.

Ad Blockers

Your tracking server can be on the same root domain as your website. For example, you can use a subdomain like collect.mywebsite.com. That way information sent from the browser to your tracking server is indistinguishable from any regular information flow your website uses. 

That makes it harder for ad blockers to outright block it (at least for now).

Browsers’ Restrictions

Additionally, since your tracking server is on the same root domain as your website, it can create first-party cookies too. This goes around the restrictions put in place by browsers around third-party cookies.

It’s also possible to make your server-side tracking implementation foolproof against some of the browsers’ restrictions targeting first-party cookies.

Regulatory Compliance

Since data is first sent to your server before reaching vendors’ servers, you can ensure no unwanted data is sent to third parties. When you use a server-side tracking setup, third-party vendors only receive what you explicitly send them.

For instance, you could decide not to forward to them the user’s IP address and their user agent information. Third-party vendors have no way of independently harvesting user data as they might do in the browser without your knowledge.

Website Performance

You can dramatically cut the amount of tags loaded in the browser and improve your website performance. You’ll have fewer tags, so your pages will weigh less and load faster. You’ll also have fewer scripts sending requests to third-party servers, making your pages more responsive.

Data Quality

With server-side tracking, you can improve your data quality in multiple ways and make better decisions from it. Some aspects of it are the following...

Data Enrichment

It’s now easier to enrich your data before sending it to your analytics platform, your data warehouse, or third-party vendors. You can apply heavy-duty processing to the collected data before relaying it elsewhere.

Data Reliability

By relying less on the browser to send data, your tracking becomes less vulnerable to browser issues like HTTP request cancellations when users navigate between pages and countless network issues that can happen in the browser. You end up with less data loss overall.

Data Freshness

Since you collect user data in real-time on a server you control, you can use the data right away, for dashboarding, monitoring, real-time personalization, or reverse ETL. You don’t have to wait for your analytics or any other third-party platform to process the data before you can use it.

Okay, that’s a lot of good things. Right? With all these benefits, can we say that server-side tracking is a perfect solution?

We can surely say that server-side tracking is a better way, a future-proof way to satisfy your data collection and campaign attribution needs. But it’s not perfect. Implementing a server-side tracking setup doesn’t come without a few drawbacks.

Alas, There’s No Free Lunch: Disadvantages of Server-Side Tracking

Even though the drawbacks of implementing a server-side tracking solution do not outweigh the benefits, they are still drawbacks. You need to be aware of them to evaluate the tradeoffs and find ways to mitigate them.

The main issues with server-side tracking are complexity, costs, and time.

Complexity

The first issue is that it’s more complex than a traditional client-side tracking setup. First, you still have to keep a small part of your client-side tracking, since that’s your data collection’s entry point.

But you’ll now have to add a new setup next to it that requires more expertise to get right, and more time overall. The complexity of server-side tracking comes from the fact that you’ll need to provision servers, usually with cloud providers.

You’ll need to configure these servers. You’ll need to secure them. You’ll need to ensure they can scale when your traffic spikes. You’ll need to have a way to monitor your servers and fix things if or when they break.

Costs

The second drawback is that since it’s more complex to deploy, you’ll need additional expertise to get it done properly, which costs more. Another way it can increase your costs is that you’ll have to pay for the computing resources needed for tracking.

You’ll pay for HTTP requests sent from the browser to your servers, and also for the requests sent from your servers to other servers. You’ll also need to pay for the computing resources required to process all these requests on your servers.

Implementation Time

Lastly, since it’s a relatively new(ish) technology, configuring server-side tags is slower than doing it client-side. Tag management systems with server-side features like Google Tag Manager regularly improve their platforms so this problem will probably go away with time.

Which Tracking Method Should I Use? What Should I Do Now?

By staying with a purely client-side tracking setup, you’re facing a situation where you risk collecting less and less data to the point that your marketing efforts are hampered. You’ll also continue to deal with regulatory compliance risks that can expose you to heavy fines and public relations headaches.

On the other hand, server-side tracking has its drawbacks too: complexity, costs, and increased implementation time. But these disadvantages are completely dwarfed by the benefits it brings.

Resistance to ad blockers and browsers’ restrictions. Better website performance, better data quality with better data enrichment, and increased reliability.

So your decision should probably tend towards implementing a server-side tracking setup, as soon as feasible.

‍Server-side tracking is not only the future, it’s worth it. Right now.

Going server-side is not a weekend project and requires adequate resources and planning, as well as the right tools and implementation partners. ​
